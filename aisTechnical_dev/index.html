How to power cycle a GRAPE

Power cycling a GRAPE is useful if you have found a sensor that is not streaming and you do not know why yet. Sometimes, like with all electronics, simply turning the thing off and on again will fix all the things.

  1. Find the sensor you are having issues with in your sensor mapping document.
  2. This sensor will have an IP address and a port listed next to it. Type in this ip address in to a web browser. You will now be asked to log in to the RocketLink switch with that IP address.
  3. You have a list of folders on the left hand side.
    • Monitor and Diag -> Device front panel: Shows an image of the switch you are working on. It shows which ports are powered and which are not. Greens means data is flowing freely through the switch, black means ded.
    • Power Over Ethernet -> PoE Control: This is where you can see which ports are powered and which are not, they will be labeled “Enabled” or “Disabled” in the first columns.
  4. Access the PoE control folder and find the port your GRAPE is plugged in to. Switch the mode from “Enable” to “Disable” and hit apply.
  5. Keep the port disabled for 5-10 minutes then re-enable it.
  6. Pray to Tower Jesus that you fixed your sensor has started streaming again

PAR

The Kipp & Zonen PQS-1 PAR Quantum Sensor (PAR or PQS1) measures photosynthetically active radiation. The most important component of the sensor is the diffuser. The diffuser is where light enters the sensor so it must be kept clear of debris, be protected from scratches or residue, and remain level to take accurate and consistent measurements. A built-in bubble level and adjustment screws allow for easy leveling. Use a soft-bristled brush or damp Kimwipe to gently wipe away accumulated dirt or residue. A digital level can be used to ensure the PAR is level along North-South and East-West axes.


The PAR sensor with the central diffuser, integrated bubble level, and three leveling bolts.

The PAR produces one data stream. Raw L0 data is collected in volts (V) and L1 readings are expressed in micromoles per second and square meter (µm/m2/s). A healthy range for L1 data is between 0 and 1000. Monitor PAR values across an entire site to verify they are reading within normal values for the area.

PAR Data Streams

0 Photosynthetic Photon Flux Density µm/m2/s 0 - 1000; L1 reading

NR-01

The Huskeflux NR01 Net Radiometer (NR01) consists of four separate sensors for measuring total global and surface reflected shortwave and longwave radiation. A pair of upward and downward facing SR01 pyranometers (domed sensor, shortwave radiation) and a pair of upward and downward facing IR01 pyrgeometers (flat sensor, longwave radiation) make up the four sensors of this instrument. The pyrgeometers are also fitted with a small 1.5W heater to keep the sensors above dew point overnight (should see activity ever night), and a temperature sensor in the body used to correct irradiance measurements. A digital level should be used to ensure the NR01 is level along x- and y-axes.

HMP-155

The Vaisala HMP-155 is a humidity and temperature sensor which measures for relative humidity, air temperature, and dew point/frost point temperature. Relative humidity is expressed as a percentage: the ratio of the partial pressure of water vapor to the equilibrium vapor pressure of water at a given temperature. At 100% relative humidity the air is considered saturated and is at its dew point.

The sensor is protected by a shield sometimes referred to as a "bird feeder" to protect the sensor from precipitation and solar radiation, while providing ventilation.

HMP-155 Data Streams
Stream # Stream Name Units Healthy Range
0 Relative Humidity % 0 to 100
1 Temperature Degrees C Site-specific
2 Dew Point Degrees C Site-specific
3 Status NA 0

Barometer

The Vaisala BAROCAP digital barometer PTB330 measures barometric pressure/static atmospheric pressure. Barometric pressure is significant in influencing weather conditions as well as aqueous chemistry (e.g. the amount of gas that can dissolve in solution). Measurements of atmospheric pressure allow atmospheric gas mixing ratios to be converted into mass quantities.

The metal pressure port coming out of the bottom of the sensor is an open port to the surrounding atmosphere. Pressure changes create a measureable change in the silicon membrane which alters the sensor's capacitance. This measurement is compared to a closed reference circuit (unaffected by atmospheric pressure changes) and corrected for temperature. An LED status indicator is present on the front panel of the barometer (should usually be solid green).

Barometer Data Streams
Stream # Stream Name Units Healthy Range
0 Barometric Pressure kPa 60 - 103
1 Temperature Degrees C Site-specific
2 Status NA 0

The barometric pressure sensors rarely fail; the only documented failure causes the barometer to stream data as "0". The sensor will still be streaming data however DQ Blizzard sensor health range tests should identify this issue.

2D Anemometer

The Gill Wind Observor II sonic anemometer is used to measure two-dimensional wind speed. Wind plays an important role in atmospheric conditions. Horizontal and vertical wind are typically a function of differential heating of the Earth's surface and subsequent pressure gradients. Winds are responsible for advection of atmospheric pollutants, moiseture, heat, and momentum.

The sensor consits of a body, four spars, and ultrasonic transducers on the ends of the spars. The transducers emit and receive ultrasonic pulses between each opposing pair oriented N-S and E-W. The sensor compares travel time between waves to calculate the wind speed component in that direction.


The 2D anemometer with representation of the "red dot" on sensor body to help with installation orientation (should be facing away from the tower or met station).

At aquatic meteorological stations a single 2D anemometer is mounted 3 meters off the ground with transducers facing upward. At terrestrial sites the 2D anemometer is located at the end of each profile boom with the transducers facing downwards. The 2D wind must orient north at all sites: a tiny red dot near the label should be facing away from the tower or met station mast.

ATS

The Aspirated Air Temperature sensor (AAT) is comprised of a platinum resistance thermometer (PRT) housed inside an aspirated shield (circulating by fan in the top of the housing) to reduce radiative heat bias. PRTs use electrical resistance to measure ambient temperature. Temperature is a fundamental physical measurement and a driving factor for physical, chemical, and biological processes.

The AAT consists of a mounting plate, radiation shield, and "snout." An AAT with one PRT is an ATS (aspirated temperature single) which is present at the aquatic met stations. Tower sites may have ATTs (aspirated temperature triple) with three PRTs inside the housing.

The fan at the top of the housing pulls air through two concentric ports. Air flowing through the inner port is to remove radiative heat from the PRTs. Air flowing through the outer port is to remove air heated by solar radiation. Air should never flow out of the snout (bottom). If so, it means the fan was installed backwards.

ATS Data Streams
Stream # Stream Name Units Healthy Range
0 (PRT) Temperature Degrees C (L1) Site-specific
0 (Fan) Fan Speed RPM 4500 to 5000
1 (Fan) Turbine Speed RPM 500 to 1000

The most common failure point with the ATS is the fan assembly, the turbine tachometer. If ranges are consistently dropping below what is expected this could indicate the fan is beginning to fail or needs to be cleaned. A failing GRAPE port can also cause fan outages.


A diagram of the ATS assembly with direction of air-flow. If air is circulating the opposite direction it is likely that the fan was installed backwards.

Tipping Bucket

This sensor assembly collects any precipitation that falls through a fixed horizontal plane (the top of the white collector). The collector then funnels liquid water into the sensor. The sensor utilizes a two-sided “bucket” that will alternate tips between sides (both of similar volume). As one side of the bucket has accumulated the prescribed amount (typically about 0.5mm of liquid precipitation accumulation in the bucket, approximately equivalent to 16.125 mL for the current bucket design), the bucket tips to the other side, immediately beginning new precipitation accumulation into the opposite bucket while the previous side tips to drain.

This tipping design allows for continuous measurement of the rainfall amount and rate. The tipping mechanism is fitted with a magnet that passes by a reed switch, closing the sensing circuit with each bucket tip in either direction. The limitation of this form of collection are small precipitation events that fail to trigger the tipping mechanism, and thus, fail to collect a measurement. Capturing low-volume precipitation events may occur within a later event, or become lost due to evaporation before allowing the gauge to measure any collection.


Heated units are fitted with a heater on both the sensor body and the precipitation collector. This ensures that frozen water precipitation events (e.g. snow, hail, sleet) are melted and recorded as liquid water (ideally prior to sublimation or evaporation). Additionally, the heaters keep the internal tipping mechanism and bearing operating freely and consistently throughout the year. The heater control thermostat set point is 4.4⁰C. Once the temperature falls to the 4.4⁰C set point, the heater automatically turns on. Once it rises to the set point, the heater turns off.
